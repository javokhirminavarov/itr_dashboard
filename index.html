WCO Illicit Trade Report 2025

44.8%

of all illicit-trade cases in 2025 moved through e-commerce.

A channel, not a commodity — and it now underlies nearly half of everything Customs sees.

Scroll to read ↓

It's everywhere

E-commerce isn't a niche channel. Split 2025's caseload across the six ITR sections and every one carries an online tail — from IPR, Health & Safety at 67.8% down to AML/CTF at 1.0%. Even the floor isn't zero.

That makes it structural, not specialist. Watch each section bar break into the commodities beneath it.

But wildly uneven

44.8% is an average, and it hides more than it shows. Ranked flow by flow, e-commerce penetration runs from 81.9% for new psychoactive substances to effectively zero for hazardous waste — a spectrum, not a number.

Seven commodities clear 55%, from NPS down to khat. Then it drops off a cliff: 55% to 34% in a single step. That break, not the average, is the real dividing line.

The ceiling — if it's mailable, it ships like retail

The commodities that live online share a profile: small, high-value, parcel-ready. Drill into their sub-commodities and the shares climb higher still — urogenital medicines at 91.0%, gun silencers at 85.1%, e-cigarettes at 63.8%.

If a good fits in an envelope and carries retail margins, it moves like retail. The channel isn't chosen — the product dictates it.

The floor — physical value stays physical

Now the bottom of the spectrum: alcohol (0.5%), cash (1.0%), drones (0.8%), ODS/HFC gases (0.6%), gold (0.4%), waste (0.2%). Three things keep them offline — bulk weight, hand-to-hand value, and dangerous cargo that needs the freight lane.

The line running down the whole spectrum is physical: what a parcel can carry, and what it can't.

It rides the infrastructure

E-commerce trafficking needs e-commerce logistics. Each country here is shaded by its WCO region's average — a regional figure, not a country-level one — and even at that resolution the split is stark: the Americas at 68% against MENA at 1.5% and West & Central Africa at 0.6%.

Read it carefully: this maps parcel and postal maturity, not crime intensity. Where caseloads still skew to bulk tobacco and postal networks are thin, the online share stays low. So far, e-commerce trafficking is a high-logistics-region phenomenon.

So what

So the 44.8% average is a targeting trap. Applied uniformly, it over-invests where the trade is already physical and under-defends where it's already online. Across a 0–82% range, the single number is the wrong unit of decision — risk management has to be commodity-specific.

For the top cluster, that means moving weight from seaports and land borders to the postal and express hubs where the parcels actually travel — and treating parcel-channel capability as core Customs infrastructure, not a specialist add-on.

Source: WCO Illicit Trade Report 2025 (data year 2024).

Notes on method

  • Shares are the percentage of 2025 cases in each flow linked to e-commerce (WCO Illicit Trade Report 2025, 2024 data).
  • This spectrum shows all 21 flow-level commodities. The report's own e-commerce chart plots only eight and omits Flora at 64.2%, which outranks Cannabis (62.6%); here it sits in its true rank.
  • New psychoactive substances appear in the source as both 81.9% and 82.7%. We use 81.9% — the flow-level, cases basis shared by every other commodity here, and the figure the report's own narrative cites.
  • The regional map shades each country by its WCO region's average — a regional figure, not country-level data — on a country-to-region lookup rebuilt from public WCO regional membership (175 of 176 codes placed).
